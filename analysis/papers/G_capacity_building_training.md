# Stream G — Capacity building, training, and intervention design in the social sector

Paper-cards for prior work on cognitive load theory, AI/digital literacy, training transfer, peer-learning networks, agricultural-extension analogues, and capacity-building program evaluations — establishing the prior on which interventions plausibly close the *comprehension* bottleneck identified in F-10.

## G1 — Sweller (1988)

```yaml

verified_url: yes
```

**Citation.** Sweller, J. (1988). Cognitive load during problem solving: effects on learning. *Cognitive Science*, 12(2), 257–285.

**DOI / URL.** https://doi.org/10.1207/s15516709cog1202_4

**Sample / Method.** Theoretical synthesis with experimental evidence on means-ends problem-solving and schema acquisition.

**Key claim(s).**
- Working memory has a hard capacity limit; conventional problem-solving consumes most of it via means-ends search, leaving none for schema construction.
- Effective instruction must reduce extraneous load before learners can build durable schemas.

**Takeaway.** Foundational framing for F-10's "comprehension bottleneck" interpretation: 52.8% of Late Adopters say "don't understand AI" — a working-memory / schema-acquisition problem, not a tools problem.

**Confirms / Complicates / Contradicts.** Confirms F-10's interpretation: comprehension is the binding constraint, and CLT predicts that further tool exposure without schema scaffolding will not move adoption.

**Citation.** Sweller, J., van Merriënboer, J. J. G., & Paas, F. (2019). Cognitive architecture and instructional design: 20 years later. *Educational Psychology Review*, 31, 261–292.

**DOI / URL.** https://doi.org/10.1007/s10648-019-09465-5

**Sample / Method.** Updated theoretical synthesis incorporating evolutionary educational psychology and 20 years of empirical CLT effects.

**Key claim(s).**
- "Biologically secondary" knowledge (e.g., reading, statistics, AI literacy) requires explicit instruction; humans don't acquire it through immersion.
- Element interactivity governs intrinsic load; instructional design must sequence high-interactivity content carefully.

**Takeaway.** AI literacy is biologically secondary — predicts that "just give them ChatGPT" exposure strategies will *not* transfer in our population. F-10's comprehension gap fits this pattern.

**Confirms / Complicates / Contradicts.** Confirms F-10. Predicts that GS access to consumer LLMs (the F-8 reversal in tool counts) will not translate into adoption competence without explicit instruction.

## Stream G synthesis

The literature triangulates on three intervention design principles directly relevant to F-10's comprehension bottleneck. **First**, the comprehension gap is a *biologically secondary*, schema-acquisition problem (G1–G3, G14, G15) — meaning that exposure-based or "just give them ChatGPT" strategies will not transfer; explicit instruction with worked examples, segmenting, and learner-tailored sequencing (G4–G7) is required. **Second**, comprehension is necessary but not sufficient: training transfer fails 60–80% of the time when work-environment supports (peer support, supervisor backing, on-the-job practice) are missing (G17, G18, G20), so any AI-readiness intervention must be designed end-to-end and not as a standalone workshop. **Third**, the most cost-effective delivery model in resource-constrained settings is *cohort-based, peer-anchored, and locally-tailored* (G8–G11, G22–G24, G32) — analogous to the agricultural-extension shift from "Training-and-Visit" to farmer-field-schools and "best-fit" pluralistic advisory services. Translated to our population: the prior favors a tiered design — light-touch peer-learning networks for AI Consumers (cluster3=1) where expertise reversal cautions against re-teaching basics (G6, G7); and deeper, andragogy-aligned, problem-centered cohorts for Late Adopters (cluster3=0) anchored in their existing data work and supported by mastery-experience design (G8, G30, G31). The same evidence base predicts that infrastructure-only investments (cloud, policies) will *not* close F-10 — consistent with our finding that Late Adopters already have higher cloud_storage and data_use_policy than Skeptics yet remain stuck on comprehension.
