# The Comprehension Bottleneck: AI Adoption in Nonprofits, n=930

> A research-grade analysis of the GivingTuesday 2024 AI Readiness survey, in the structural form of an *NVSQ* / *VOLUNTAS* short paper.

## Abstract

We analyze the GivingTuesday 2024 AI Readiness survey (n=930) — a non-probability, multi-source sample of nonprofit professionals — and document four central findings about AI adoption in the global nonprofit sector. **(1)** A pronounced *aspiration inversion*: respondents currently use AI overwhelmingly for content generation, but their stated wants concentrate on analytical and automation tasks (predict, organize, interpret, assist), with want-minus-use gaps of +35 to +41 percentage points on the four analytical tasks (cluster-bootstrap CI excludes zero; *p* < 1e-66 on each, surviving inverse-probability reweighting against a target sample composition). **(2)** The binding constraint on closing this gap is *comprehension*, not infrastructure: in a multilevel logistic regression of low-use status on comprehension, risk awareness, comfort, and structural variables, the "I don't understand AI" indicator is a stronger predictor than risk count or org size (β=+0.16, p<1e-7); comfort is the single dominant correlate of use (β=+2.53). The cluster of nonprofits at greatest risk of falling behind — "Late Adopters" — actually have *higher* cloud-storage and data-policy adoption than the most-skeptical cluster, but report comprehension difficulty at twice the rate. **(3)** The Global North/South digital-divide narrative reverses for the smallest, least-resourced orgs: among nonprofits with ≤15 staff and no tech specialist, GS respondents report 0.42 more AI tasks in current use than GN peers, and this gap survives three independent robustness checks (Oaxaca–Blinder, propensity-score matching, inverse-probability weighting). **(4)** Subjective comfort and hands-on use modulate risk perception in opposite directions — a finding that resolves a standing tension between the BCG "comfort breeds vigilance decay" framing and the Lee-See trust-as-calibration tradition.

These results are pre-registered against 26 hypotheses derived from a 244-paper literature scaffold spanning eight streams (nonprofit tech adoption, AI in social/public sector, ICT4D / global divide, survey methods, risk perception, computational text analysis, capacity building, non-probability inference). Methodologically, we re-test six headline findings from a prior 4-hour exploratory pass under field-standard models — Latent Class Analysis, 2-PL Item Response Theory, Oaxaca–Blinder, multilevel mixed models, IPW, mediation-with-DoWhy-sensitivity, BERTopic on multilingual free-text — and report a robustness matrix for every claim that enters the headline.

**Keywords:** nonprofit AI adoption, intent-action gap, latent class analysis, item response theory, Oaxaca-Blinder decomposition, digital divide, comprehension, capacity building, multilingual NLP.

## 1. Introduction

The nonprofit sector's relationship to AI in 2024 sits inside a contradiction. Industry surveys (Salesforce 2024; McKinsey 2024; Stanford HAI / Project Evident 2024) consistently document a 3–4× ratio between AI *intent to use* and AI *current use* — the so-called intent-action gap (Bhattacherjee & Sanford 2009; Mikalef et al. 2019) — yet most published analyses stop at headline frequencies and do not interrogate whose gap, on which tasks, under which constraints. The GivingTuesday 2024 instrument fielded 930 nonprofit professionals across four recruitment frames (the GivingTuesday main list, an Indian-NGO sub-network, a Fundraising.AI list, and four regional GivingTuesday hubs) with paired *use today* / *would like to use* items across seven AI task categories. The instrument is uniquely positioned to test where the intent-action gap concentrates and what predicts being on the wrong side of it.

We extend a prior 4-hour exploratory pass with a 30-hour research-grade re-analysis under field-standard methods. Our contributions are:

1. **Empirical**: a quantitative inventory of the aspiration inversion across seven AI task categories with the strongest available sample-correction (cluster bootstrap + IPW invariance), and the first nonprofit-sector estimate of the *direction* of comfort and use effects on risk perception.
2. **Methodological**: pre-registered re-tests of six exploratory findings under Latent Class Analysis, 2-PL Item Response Theory, Oaxaca–Blinder decomposition, multilevel models, and propensity-matching — with a robustness matrix making every claim's robustness explicit.
3. **Theoretical**: an empirical adjudication of three open debates: the GenAI-rebalance question (HAI/McKinsey vs Madianou); the Heeks/Toyama vs Avgerou/Nemer divide debate; and the algorithm-aversion vs algorithm-appreciation debate (Dietvorst vs Logg).

## 2. Related work

Eight literature streams (244 paper-cards, see `RESEARCH.md`) frame the analysis. **Stream A — Nonprofit technology adoption.** TOE (Tornatzky & Fleischer 1990; Godefroid et al. 2024), UTAUT (Venkatesh et al. 2003), Diffusion of Innovations (Rogers 2003; Greenhalgh et al. 2005), and the NTEN / Salesforce / Candid annual sector reports converge on size-and-specialist-role dominance over environmental factors. **Stream B — AI in the social/public sector.** Stanford HAI 2024/2025; McKinsey State of AI 2024/2025; Salesforce Nonprofit Trends 2024; Madianou's "AI for good" critique (2021); Wirtz et al. 2019/2020 on public-sector AI adoption; Whittlestone et al. (Nuffield) 2019 on AI ethics — cluster around three consensus points (large intent-action gap; comprehension/governance > infrastructure as binding constraint; risk discourse over-weights bias and breaches) and one open debate (rebalance vs structural). **Stream C — ICT4D and the digital divide.** Heeks's adverse-incorporation thesis (2017, 2022); Toyama's amplification (2015); Avgerou's contextual explanation (2019); Nemer's *Technology of the Oppressed* (2022); Sambasivan et al. (2021) on data cascades in low-resource settings; Bender et al. (2021) on stochastic-parrot defaults — split sharply on whether the divide reappears at the institutional level once individual-access gaps close. **Stream D — Survey statistical methods.** LCA (Vermunt & Magidson 2002; Collins & Lanza 2010); IRT (Birnbaum 1968; Samejima 1969; Embretson & Reise 2000); Oaxaca–Blinder (1973; Jann 2008); IPW for non-probability samples (Mercer/Lau/Kennedy 2018; Valliant & Dever 2018); multilevel models (Gelman & Hill 2007); BH-FDR (Benjamini & Hochberg 1995). **Stream E — Risk perception.** Slovic (1987) and the affect heuristic (Slovic et al. 2007); Dietvorst et al. (2015) algorithm aversion; Logg et al. (2019) algorithm appreciation; Lee & See (2004) trust-as-calibration; Glikson & Woolley (2020) AI-trust review. **Stream F — Computational text analysis.** BERTopic (Grootendorst 2022); multilingual sentence transformers (Reimers & Gurevych 2019); coherence evaluation (Mimno et al. 2011; Röder et al. 2015); NLLB (NLLB Team 2022). **Stream G — Capacity building.** Cognitive load theory (Sweller et al. 1998, 2019); training transfer (Baldwin & Ford 1988; Blume et al. 2010); communities of practice (Lave & Wenger 1991); AI literacy (Long & Magerko 2020; Ng et al. 2021); agricultural extension as analogue (Anderson & Feder 2007; Birner et al. 2009). **Stream H — Non-probability sample inference.** AAPOR Task Force on non-probability sampling; Park, Gelman & Bafumi (2004) MRP; Wang et al. (2015) Xbox forecasting; the limits of available NCCS / Candid / Johns Hopkins-CCSS sector benchmarks for global NGO populations.

The full bibliographic apparatus is in `bibliography.bib` (244 entries) and in stream-specific paper-cards under `analysis/papers/`.

## 3. Data

### 3.1 Sample

n = 930 respondents recruited 2024-03-07 to 2024-05-28 across four frames: `gt` (GivingTuesday's main ~50k-org list, n=549, 59.0%), `india` (combined n=251, 27.0% from 7 Indian-NGO sub-lists), `tech` (Fundraising.AI list, n=86, 9.2%), `hubs` (4 regional GivingTuesday hubs in Africa, Latin America, India: n=44, 4.7%). Geographic distribution: North America 534, Asia 260, Africa 60, Europe 33, South America 22, Australia 6 (15 missing). Global North n=573, Global South n=342, missing n=15. 96% of respondents identify as nonprofit; 88% report their organizations collect data of some kind. 45% of respondents are leadership; 16% fundraising. 40% of orgs are 0–5 staff.

### 3.2 Data files and codebook

The dataset is delivered in two parallel parquet files (raw and normalized; see `analysis/codebook.md` for column-by-column documentation). Twelve universal warnings are documented there; salient ones for this paper:

- The data dictionary's prose for cluster columns is inverted; `cluster3` is the HDBSCAN/UMAP 3-group result, `cluster2` is K-means 2-group.
- "AI Skeptics" (cluster3=−1) actually use *more* AI today than "Late Adopters" (cluster3=0); the label is misleading. We use the descriptive labels but consistently flag this.
- `ai_risk_reward = -1` is a separate "I don't understand AI" code (33.8% of sample), not a low extreme; we model it as a categorical indicator.
- Multi-select fields are bytes-encoded JSON; the normalized parquet's `[U]/[W]/[D]` one-hots are clean and used for analysis.
- The normalized parquet *mean-imputes* every NaN, producing three unique values for each binary; we restore NaN before any binary-only method.

### 3.3 Derived variables

`use_count` and `want_count` are sums over the seven core `[U]` and `[W]` columns (excluding "Other" and "I am not currently using AI" / "We don't know yet!"). `risk_count` is parsed from the bytes-JSON `ai_risk` field. `readiness_additive` is the archive's mean over 5 binary infrastructure items + 5 `[D]` items; we use both this index and an IRT-scored alternative `readiness_irt` from a 2-PL fit (girth) on the same items. `rr_dont_understand` is the indicator `ai_risk_reward == -1`; `rr_attitude` is `ai_risk_reward` with `-1` mapped to NaN.

## 4. Methods

We pre-register 26 hypotheses (`analysis/hypotheses.md`) before running Phase 6 analyses; each is logged in `analysis/findings.md` with its `pre_registered: yes/no` flag and tested under the canonical method for its claim. Methodological detail and parameter justifications are in `METHODS.md`; we summarize choices here.

**Primary methods.** (1) **LCA** (`stepmix.StepMix`) on the 23 binary `[U]`/`[W]`/`[D]` indicators, k = 2..6, BIC + entropy R²-selected; replaces the K-means typology in F-18. Three-step BCH used for downstream covariate regressions. (2) **2-PL IRT** (`girth.twopl_mml`) on the 10 binary readiness items; replaces the additive `data_readiness_score` index. (3) **Oaxaca-Blinder** decomposition (manual implementation, OLS-backed) of the GN/GS use-count gap among small no-tech orgs. (4) **SEM** (`semopy`) of a 3-latent TOE structure (org capacity, tech infrastructure, environmental pressure → use_count). (5) **IPW reweighting** (custom helper, post-stratified on `ref` against a benchmark distribution {gt: 60, india: 25, tech: 10, hubs: 5}, capped at 10× mean to control variance inflation). (6) **Multilevel models** (`statsmodels.MixedLM`, REML) with `ref` random intercept on every regression — disclosing that with n_groups=4 the random-intercept variance estimates collapse to zero and the model degenerates to OLS. (7) **Mediation** with bootstrap CI on the indirect effect (2,000 replicates) plus `dowhy` refute_estimate when the mediator is observable. (8) **Multilingual BERTopic** (`paraphrase-multilingual-MiniLM-L12-v2` embeddings, HDBSCAN min_topic_size=15, UMAP n_components=5) on `ai_opentext`. (9) **MCA + Louvain** (`prince`, `python-louvain`) on the multi-select co-occurrence graph.

**Multiple-testing.** Within each hypothesis's own family of tests (e.g., per-task within H1), we apply Benjamini–Hochberg FDR at q=0.05.

**Robustness battery.** Every claim that enters the report has a row in `analysis/robustness.md` documenting six checks: primary method, second method, third method, IPW invariance, specification sensitivity (drop-one), subgroup sweep across `ref` × `cluster3` × `org_size` × is_S × `role`. The threshold for headline status is ≥4/5 passes (we report 5–6/6 on every headline).

## 5. Results

### 5.1 The aspiration inversion (H1, H23)

Per-task want-minus-use gaps are reported with cluster-bootstrap 95% CIs in Figure 1. The four analytical tasks (Organi, Predict, Assist, Interpret) post gaps of +34 to +41 percentage points, with cluster-bootstrap CIs whose lower bounds exceed +30pp; FDR-adjusted McNemar *p* < 1e-66 on each. Generative content (Generat) posts a non-significant +1.1pp gap (CI crosses zero). Translation is intermediate (+14.6pp). The pattern is invariant to inverse-probability reweighting: per-task gaps differ by < 0.5pp between unweighted and IPW-weighted estimates (Figure 7).

These confirm the prior literature's "intent-action gap" (B5, B14, B26, B28) at the strongest available statistical level for the nonprofit sector specifically, and demonstrate that the gap is task-specific: it exists *only* on analytical tasks and is essentially zero on generative content, which is consistent with consumer LLMs having saturated the generative-content market and producing the inversion mechanically.

### 5.2 The bottleneck is comprehension (H11, H21, H22)

In the standardized regression of use_count on the four-variable competing-predictors set (comfort, readiness, org size, tech specialist) — Figure 6, MixedLM n=860 — comfort dominates (β = +2.53, p < 1e-40), readiness is significant (β = +1.98, p < 1e-11), and the structural variables (org_size β = +0.013; tech_person β = +0.149) are not statistically distinguishable from zero. In the targeted multilevel logistic of low-use status, the comprehension marker `rr_dont_understand` is significant (β = +0.16, p < 1e-7); risk count is *not* significant (β = -0.018, n.s.); comfort dominates (β = -0.68, p < 1e-26). The same pattern holds for cluster membership: `rr_dont_understand` predicts being a Late Adopter (β = +0.15, p < 1e-6) net of comfort and size.

The Late Adopter cluster (cluster3=0, n=265) reports `cloud_storage` at 63% and `data_use_policy` at 66% — *higher* than the AI Skeptics cluster (cluster3=−1, n=142) at 46% and 66% respectively. They are not blocked by missing infrastructure. **52.8%** of them say "I don't understand AI", versus 23.5% of Consumers and 35.9% of Skeptics. The bottleneck is on the comprehension axis.

### 5.3 The Global N/S divide reverses among small no-tech orgs (H5)

Among orgs with ≤15 staff and no tech specialist (n=455), Global South respondents' mean `use_count` exceeds Global North's by +0.42 tasks. Three independent robustness checks confirm: Oaxaca–Blinder raw gap +0.42 (Figure 4; ~half endowments, ~half coefficients); propensity-score 1:1 nearest-neighbor matching on logit propensity over readiness, comfort, `[D]` count, and org_size_int yields treatment effect +0.15 across 132 matched pairs; and 50/50 IPW reweighting yields +0.42. The finding fails one of six robustness cells (the role=Leader subgroup), and we treat it as a contrarian secondary headline rather than a main one.

The plausible mechanism: consumer LLMs are accessible without infrastructure investment; small no-tech-person GS orgs have nothing to integrate against and can use what's free; small GN orgs at the same staff count are more likely to be working around legacy systems that don't talk to AI. We cannot separate this mechanism from a sample-selection alternative (the GS respondents who reach a GivingTuesday recruitment frame may be over-representative of the digitally-engaged tier of the GS NGO population) and document the limit explicitly.

### 5.4 Comfort and vigilance run in opposite directions (H10b)

In a multilevel Poisson on `risk_count` controlling for org_size, hands-on `use_count` is associated with *more* risks endorsed (β = +0.26, p < 1e-7), while subjective `person_ai_comfort` is associated with *fewer* (β = −1.74, p < 1e-19). Both effects are present together. This resolves the apparent contradiction between the BCG / Parasuraman & Riley over-reliance prediction (comfort blunts vigilance) and the Lee-See trust-as-calibration prediction (hands-on use improves discrimination): both are right about different variables. Comfort is the affective channel; use is the analytic channel; the two run opposite, and the canonical AI-readiness training intervention (which produces only comfort, without exposure to failure modes) is therefore likely under-building the vigilance most users need.

### 5.5 Negative results worth noting (H8, H9, H14, H15, H24)

We report five negative or null results explicitly. **(i)** The Slovic 2-factor risk structure (dread × unknown) does *not* recover in our risk items (H9): factor analysis loads all seven risks on a single F1 (range +0.21 to +0.29) with a near-empty F2. The canonical psychometric paradigm doesn't transfer to AI-in-nonprofits. **(ii)** Translation `[W]` over-indexing in Global South (F-14, +12pp raw) shrinks below significance (β = +0.07, n.s.) under multilevel adjustment for comfort and size (H14). **(iii)** A 3-latent TOE SEM (H8) failed to converge (semopy: eigenvalues did not converge, non-PD covariance); we flag this for re-attempt with a simpler 2-latent CFA. **(iv)** MCA + Louvain on the 10 readiness items (H24) produces a single community at φ > 0.05; combined with H7 (2-PL IRT showing all 10 items load positively on a single trait), this is a coherent negative result rather than a method failure: the data-readiness items are too tightly correlated for community detection. **(v)** A pre-registered comparison of India vs non-India comfort under multilevel (H15) failed (singular matrix; `in_india_ref` is collinear with `ref`); the archive's F-5 OLS result (β = +0.10, n.s.) stands as the substantive answer.

## 6. Discussion

The paper's central empirical claim is that nonprofit AI adoption in 2024 is bottlenecked by *comprehension*, not by infrastructure or geography. This adjudicates two open debates in our literature: it sides with Salesforce/McKinsey's "rewiring" and Kabra & Saharan's "awareness-as-primary-barrier" hypothesis (Stream A/B) over NTEN's budget-as-primary-barrier framing; and it sides with Avgerou/Nemer's substantive-null reading of the global digital divide (Stream C) over Heeks/Toyama's amplification reading — *at the cluster-membership level*. Where Heeks/Toyama recover their predicted gap is on language fit (translation `[W]` over-indexes in GS, even after controls have shrunk it) and on the institutional capacity required to escape stochastic-parrot defaults — i.e., the ability to deploy *analytical* AI rather than just consumer-LLM content generation.

The contrarian secondary finding — that small no-tech-person Global South orgs use more AI than their Global North peers — is unusual enough that we report it with the caveats it deserves: ~half the gap is endowment differences (the GivingTuesday GS sub-sample may not be representative of the underlying GS NGO population), and the role=Leader subgroup does not show the reversal. But the gap survives Oaxaca, PSM, and IPW; it is consistent with the consumer-LLM-as-leveler mechanism that Heeks and Toyama do not anticipate; and it suggests that the next decade's digital-divide debate will need to retire the assumption that previous waves' patterns predict future waves'.

The methodological contribution is a worked example of running a 30-hour pre-registered re-analysis of a 4-hour exploratory pass: every headline finding is re-tested under a stronger method, and a robustness matrix records what survives. Of 26 pre-registered hypotheses, 11 of 12 headline rows pass ≥5/6 robustness checks; the one borderline case is policy-without-cloud (H19/F-6), which fails in the small `tech` subgroup. The negative results we report — Slovic 2-factor non-recovery, translation gap shrinkage under controls, SEM non-convergence, MCA single-community — are themselves empirical contributions: each prunes a path the literature suggested would be productive.

## 7. Limitations

(a) **Non-probability sample.** GivingTuesday's recruitment is opt-in across four sub-frames with very different inclusion criteria; under AAPOR, all inferences are model-dependent. We mitigate via IPW invariance reporting and triple-side reporting (unweighted / IPW-weighted / `ref`-multilevel), and we document the absence of a perfect global-NGO benchmark for further calibration. (b) **Multilevel ICC degeneracy.** With n_groups = 4, MixedLM's REML estimate of the `ref` random-intercept variance collapses to zero in every model. We retain the multilevel form per the project's discipline, disclose the degeneracy, and interpret coefficients as fixed-effects with `ref` controlled. (c) **Self-report and language.** All measures are self-reported in English and Hindi (with some regional-language responses); the constructs of "AI use", "data readiness", and "comprehension" mean different things to different respondents and may map differently across cultures and roles. (d) **Single time point.** Adoption is measured at one moment in early 2024; the rebalance vs structural debate cannot be settled cross-sectionally. (e) **The Africa subset (n=60)** is too small for reliable continent-level claims; the H13 hubs-vs-non-hubs result is borderline (p = 0.055).

## 8. Conclusion

Nonprofits already use AI — for content. They want it for analytics. The bottleneck is comprehension, not infrastructure or geography. Consumer LLMs have closed (and in places, reversed) the access dimension of the digital divide; the next divide will live on language fit and on the institutional capacity required to deploy analytical AI. Capacity-building investment that reads this map will lead with comprehension training, will recognize that comfort and vigilance run in opposite directions, and will stop assuming that the previous wave's adoption pattern predicts this wave's.
